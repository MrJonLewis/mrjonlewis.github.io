Hosting a meeting
Holding a meeting can (deceptively) feel like progress even if none is made.

Alternatives to meetings

  • Consider a small workshop with just one person
  • Write down the questions you have and see if answers emerge over time
  • If the goal is to share knowledge then create a video or short article/email to share
  • If the goal is to gather opinion
  • Ask the question in a relevant slack channel
  • Send out a short survey
  • Consider meeting with several people 1:1
    • This can take more of your time but less of other's
    • You can get deeper insights and more open conversation
    • It’s easier to address individual concerns in this context than in a group
  • Get status updates via slack or on jira
  • Do nothing for a while and see what happens!

Before

You might need to spend more time prepping for the meeting than running the meeting itself.
  • Set a goal for the meeting
    • The expected outcomes
    • How it aligns with the company purposes (?link??)
  • Who is facilitating?
  • Who is the scribe?
    • Agree the level of detail ahead of time.
  • Will this be in person, remote or a mixture of both?
    • Indicate clearly in the meeting title
  • Attendees
    • Invite attendees using Google calendar
    • Use the notes field to share
      • The goal for the meeting
      • Why they have been invited/how they are expected to contribute
      • Any preparation they need to do for the meeting

Agenda design

A full HBR Article on agenda design

  • Seek input from team members. If you want your team to be engaged in meetings, make sure the agenda includes items that reflect their needs.
  • Select topics that affect the entire team. They are also likely to be issues for which people have different information and needs.
  • List agenda topics as questions the team needs to answer. For example: “project technical approach” should be “what type of technical integration meets the need for the project and budget?”
  • Even better, bring proposals to the meeting rather than questions. A couple of people working on a proposal before a meeting may shorten the meeting overall. Use consent decision making to amend the proposal if needed.
  • Note whether the purpose of the topic is to share information, seek input for a decision, or make a decision.
  • Estimate a realistic amount of time for each topic.
  • Propose a process for addressing each agenda item.
    • Consent decision making
    • Consensus
    • Delegation poker
    • Lightning decision jam
      • A standard format
      • Get all the relevant information on the table. (10 mins)
      • Identify and agree on any assumptions we need to make. (10 mins)
      • Identify and agree on the interests/needs that should be met for any solution. (10 mins)
      • Craft a solution that ideally takes into account all the interests/needs, and is consistent with relevant information and assumptions. (15 mins)
      • Ask for any suggestions for improving the process
  • Specify how members should prepare for the meeting. Distribute the agenda with sufficient time before the meeting, so the team can read background materials and prepare their initial thoughts for each agenda item ahead of time.
  • Identify who is responsible for leading each topic.
  • Make the first topic “review and modify agenda as needed.”

Tips and links

Meetings are good for

  • Securing multiple perspectives on sticky issues
  • Surfacing and processing tensions around proposed changes
  • Personal and team development
  • Making sure people are heard
  • Process improvement as a group

Keeping it efficient

  • Book meetings for 20 or 40 minutes rather than 30 or 60.
  • Try holding virtual meetings rather than in-person if it’s short
  • Do walking or stand up meetings
  • Does this have to be a real-time meeting or can it work asynchronously?
